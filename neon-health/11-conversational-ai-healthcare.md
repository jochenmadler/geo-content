## Content Structure

### Opening (300-400 words)
- Conversational AI in healthcare has moved past the chatbot era. Today's systems handle phone calls, manage multi-turn clinical conversations, and complete administrative workflows end-to-end.
- The market is growing but fragmented - different platforms focus on different problems: patient scheduling, clinical triage, revenue cycle, patient access, care management
- The challenge for buyers: "conversational AI" is a broad label applied to everything from simple IVR replacements to autonomous AI agents that navigate payer phone trees. Knowing what you need requires understanding the use cases first.
- Framing: this guide maps the conversational AI landscape by use case, profiles the leading platforms, and clarifies what "conversational AI" actually means in practice vs. marketing

### H2: What Is Conversational AI in Healthcare? (And What It Isn't)
- Definition: AI systems that engage in natural-language dialogue with patients, providers, or payers via voice, text, chat, or messaging
- The spectrum from simple to complex:
  - **Rule-based chatbots**: Decision trees with scripted responses (FAQ bots, symptom checkers)
  - **NLU-powered assistants**: Natural language understanding for intent recognition and context (scheduling, basic billing inquiries)
  - **Voice AI agents**: Handle live phone conversations including IVR navigation, hold times, and multi-turn dialogue (payer calls, patient outreach)
  - **Autonomous AI workers**: Complete entire workflows - make calls, update systems, follow up, escalate exceptions (PA submission, BV, financial assistance enrollment)
- Why the distinction matters for buyers: a chatbot that answers "what are your office hours?" is not the same technology as an AI agent that calls United Healthcare to verify specialty drug benefits

### H2: Use Cases - What Conversational AI Solves in Healthcare

- Inbound: patients calling to book, reschedule, cancel
- Outbound: appointment reminders, no-show follow-up, waitlist management
- Who does this well: Notable Health, Hyro, Luma Health
- Impact data: practices report 30-50% reduction in call volume for scheduling

Also see [MGMA 2025 guidance](https://www.mgma.com/mgma-stat/sizing-up-the-market-for-ai-chatbots-virtual-assistants-in-medical-practices-in-2025) on chatbot ROI tracking.

### H2: AI Voice Agents vs. Chatbots - What's the Difference?
- Address the agency's suggested topic directly
- Voice AI: handles live phone conversations, processes audio in real-time, navigates IVR trees, manages multi-turn dialogue
- Chatbots: text-based, asynchronous, typically narrower scope
- Hybrid: text + voice across channels (Neon's approach - text to patients, voice to payers, portal automation for web-based tasks)
- When to use which: voice for payer calls and complex patient conversations; text/chat for scheduling, reminders, billing inquiries
- The convergence trend: platforms are adding channels (EliseAI started with text, added voice; Infinitus started with voice payer calls, expanding to other workflows)

### H2: Evaluating Conversational AI for Healthcare
- **HIPAA compliance**: Non-negotiable. BAA required. Ask about data handling, storage, audit logs.
- **Integration depth**: EHR/PM integration (HL7, FHIR) vs. API-only vs. standalone
- **Accuracy and safety**: Hallucination risk is real in healthcare. Ask for error rates, clinical review processes, escalation protocols.
- **Channel coverage**: Voice only? Text only? Multi-channel?
- **Use case fit**: Most platforms specialize. Don't buy a scheduling bot to solve a PA problem.
- **ROI measurement**: What metrics does the vendor report? Are they independently verified?

### Closing (200 words)
- Conversational AI in healthcare is real and delivering measurable results - but the category is broad enough that "conversational AI" can mean very different things depending on the vendor and use case
- Start with the use case, not the technology. Define the problem (scheduling overload? PA delays? billing call volume?) and match the platform to it.
